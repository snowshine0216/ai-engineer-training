## Summary of Findings

### 1. Significant Parameters & Effects
**Chunk Size** and **Splitter Type** are the primary drivers of performance.
*   **Chunk Size**: Small chunks (e.g., Token size 32) perform poorly because they often fail to capture the complete semantic meaning required for accurate retrieval. Performance improves significantly as chunk size increases, stabilizing around 256-512 tokens.
*   **Splitter Type**: Sentence-based splitters generally outperform arbitrary token splitters at finding complete information because they respect linguistic boundaries, ensuring that semantic units are not fractured.

### 2. Pros & Cons of Chunk Overlap
*   **Too Small**: Risks missing information that spans across the cut points. Although the `overlap=0` sentence split performed well here, in general contexts, zero overlap can lead to disconnected context and lower retrieval quality for queries focusing on boundary information.
*   **Too Large**: Increases redundancy without proportionally improving accuracy. As seen with `overlap=200` and `overlap=50%`, high overlap often leads to retrieving multiple chunks containing the same information, which wastes the LLM's token budget.

### 3. Trade-off: Precise Retrieval vs. Context Richness
To balance these competing needs:
*   **Optimal Chunk Sizing**: Use a moderate chunk size (e.g., 256-512 tokens) or sentence-based chunking. This is large enough to provide context but small enough to be specific.
*   **Sentence Window Retrieval**: This is a powerful strategy to resolve the trade-off. It indexes small, precise units (single sentences) for **high-precision retrieval**, but delivers a larger window of surrounding sentences to the LLM for **context richness**. This method consistently achieved high scores (5/5) across accuracy and completeness in the experiments.
